Event stream

The moral and operational difference

Cyber crime extracts value. Cybersecurity preserves value.

The same technical domain can be used for opposite purposes. The deciding line is authorization, intent, proportionality, legality and impact.

Cyber crime

Coercion, theft and disruption

  • Intent: profit, espionage, coercion, sabotage or influence.
  • Method: exploit trust, weak identity controls, unpatched systems and dependency chains.
  • Business effect: downtime, ransom pressure, fraud, privacy loss, legal cost and reputational injury.
  • Human effect: stress, delayed services, financial harm, loss of confidence and sometimes safety risk.
VS
Cybersecurity

Protection, verification and resilience

  • Intent: protect people, data, operations and trust.
  • Method: reduce attack surface, verify identity, isolate critical systems and monitor behavior.
  • Business effect: continuity, compliance, safer innovation and faster recovery.
  • Human effect: confidence that essential services will keep working when criminals apply pressure.
Incidents that changed boardrooms

When cyber risk left the server room.

These incidents are included for awareness, not imitation. The lesson is defensive: build systems that can absorb failure without collapsing the mission.

2021 · Colonial Pipeline

Fuel distribution became a cyber issue

A ransomware attack led Colonial Pipeline to proactively shut down its pipeline system; CISA described the event as a watershed moment that made critical-infrastructure cyber risk a “kitchen table issue.”

SectorEnergyFailure modeRansomwareLessonMFA + segmentation

Consciousness point: a password and remote access weakness can ripple into fuel queues, emergency coordination and public anxiety.

2024 · Change Healthcare

Healthcare payments became patient risk

The attack disrupted healthcare operations nationally, affecting eligibility checks, prescriptions, claims and payments. AHA reported that 74% of surveyed hospitals saw direct patient-care impact and 94% financial impact.

SectorHealthcareFailure modeRansomware + supplier dependencyLessonVendor resilience

Consciousness point: when a hidden digital intermediary fails, hospitals, pharmacies and patients feel it immediately.

2023 · MGM Resorts

A phone call shook a hospitality empire

Public reporting links the MGM disruption to social engineering against support processes. Systems for hotels, casinos, payments and internal operations were disrupted, with MGM later reporting about $100M in impact.

SectorHospitalityFailure modeIdentity compromiseLessonHelpdesk verification

Consciousness point: attackers did not need to “break the machine” first; they manipulated the human process around it.

2017 · Maersk / NotPetya

Global shipping learned blast radius

NotPetya crippled global IT operations for major organizations. Maersk publicly estimated a $200M–$300M impact and the case became a reference point for segmentation, backups and recovery strategy.

SectorShippingFailure modeWiper malwareLessonRecovery architecture

Consciousness point: a company can become collateral damage in a geopolitical cyber event and still have to keep the world moving.

Why businesses lose

The attacker’s equation

  • Asymmetry: the criminal needs one path; the defender is accountable for thousands.
  • Time pressure: downtime turns technical incidents into executive crises.
  • Data leverage: stolen data becomes extortion, fraud, regulatory exposure and customer fear.
  • Dependency chains: a supplier can become the shortest path into your business impact.
Why defenders win

The resilience equation

  • Identity certainty: phishing-resistant MFA, device trust and strict support verification.
  • Blast-radius control: least privilege, segmentation and isolated backups.
  • Early detection: security telemetry focused on behavior, not just known malware.
  • Practiced recovery: crisis roles, legal coordination, communication templates and tested restoration.
From fear to capability

A resilience architecture people can understand.

No organization can prevent every attempted attack. Mature cybersecurity turns inevitable pressure into survivable incidents.

1

See

Know the assets, users, suppliers, data and business processes that matter most.

2

Harden

Patch exposed systems, remove unused access, enforce MFA/passkeys and secure cloud defaults.

3

Observe

Collect logs from identity, endpoints, email, cloud, network and critical applications.

4

Contain

Segment critical systems, isolate compromised users, revoke tokens and rotate secrets.

5

Recover

Restore from tested backups, verify integrity, communicate clearly and learn publicly where appropriate.

The most important control is not a tool. It is accountability: leaders who treat cybersecurity as a duty of care, engineers who build safer defaults, employees who question suspicious pressure, and organizations that practice recovery before the crisis.
